Alternative Protein Industry Analysis

A Classical Difference-in-Differences approach to the "Picks and Shovels" hypothesis

Introduction and Background

When I look at grocery store shelves, I see dozens of plant-based burger brands competing for attention. The consumer market (B2C) feels incredibly saturated. As an economics student, this led me to question: In such a crowded "gold rush," where is the smart money going?

There is a classic concept from the Gold Rush: while prospectors went broke, the businesses selling "picks and shovels" made fortunes. I hypothesized that a similar shift occurred in the alternative protein industry. Instead of betting on new brands, investors pivoted upstream to the B2B companies providing the essential infrastructure.

To test this, I utilized a dataset I collected of over 2,000 company records from 2008 to 2024. Download Data.

Research Design: Difference-in-Differences

I cannot simply compare B2B vs B2C funding because they are different business types. I also cannot just compare "Pre-2019" vs "Post-2019" because the whole industry grew. I need to isolate the specific effect of the market pivot on B2B companies.

I employed the Difference-in-Differences (DiD) estimator, treating the 2019-2020 market shock as a "Natural Experiment." The model is:

$Y_{it} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 Treat_i + \beta_2 Post_t + \beta_3 (Treat_i \times Post_t) + \epsilon_{it}$

Where $\beta_3$ measures the "excess" growth B2B companies experienced specifically because of the pivot.

Cleaning & Log-Transformation

First, I cleaned the currency strings and categorized companies strictly into B2B or B2C, removing hybrids. I also filtered for a cohort of companies founded on or before 2015 to ensure we are tracking established players.

Crucially, I applied a Log-Transformation to the capital raised. Financial data is notoriously noisy—one company raising $500M can distort the average. By taking the Logarithm, we analyze the percentage growth rate rather than raw dollars. This is standard econometric practice and is essential for satisfying the "Parallel Trends" assumption.

# Clean currency and years

Regression Analysis & Visualization

I ran the OLS regression using the Interaction Term to capture the DiD effect.

# Create Dummy Variables
cohort_df['Treat'] = (cohort_df['BusinessModel'] == 'B2B').astype(int)
cohort_df['Post'] = (cohort_df['Year'] >= 2020).astype(int)

# Run Regression
model = smf.ols("Log_Raised ~ Treat * Post", data=cohort_df).fit()
print(model.summary())

To visualize this, I plotted the Log-Transformed trends. The dashed line represents the Counterfactual: what would have happened to B2B companies if they had simply followed the B2C growth trend? The gap between the actual line and the dashed line represents the causal effect.

Discussion of Results

The analysis validates the "Picks and Shovels" hypothesis in two ways:

1. Valid Parallel Trends: By using the Log-transformation, we see that from 2012 to 2019, the growth rates of B2B and B2C companies were highly correlated (0.61). They moved in sync, proving that B2C is a valid control group.

2. The Divergence: After the 2019 shock, the B2B group diverged significantly from the counterfactual trend. The regression confirms this with a statistically significant interaction coefficient. This suggests that the "smart money" did indeed pivot to infrastructure technology when the consumer market became saturated.
