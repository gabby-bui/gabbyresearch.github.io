Alternative Protein Industry Analysis

A Classical Difference-in-Differences approach to the "Picks and Shovels" hypothesis

Introduction and Background

When I look at the grocery store shelves these days, I see dozens of plant-based burger brands competing for attention. From Beyond Meat to Impossible Foods and countless others, the consumer market (B2C) feels incredibly saturated. As an economics student, this observation led me to a question about capital allocation: In such a crowded "gold rush," where is the smart money actually going?

There is a classic concept from the 1849 Gold Rush known as the "picks and shovels" strategy. History tells us that while most prospectors digging for gold went broke, the businesses selling them tools—like Levis Strauss selling jeans—made consistent fortunes. I hypothesized that a similar structural shift occurred in the alternative protein industry around 2019. Instead of betting on yet another burger brand, investors likely pivoted upstream to the B2B companies providing the essential ingredients and infrastructure to the entire sector.

To test this, I utilized a dataset I personally collected and cleaned, containing over 2,000 company records from 2008 to 2024. You can access the raw data here.

Research Design: The Difference-in-Differences Estimator

To rigorously test this hypothesis, I cannot simply compare the average funding of B2B versus B2C companies, as they are fundamentally different businesses with different capital needs. Nor can I simply compare funding "before and after" 2019, as the entire industry grew during this period due to macro-economic factors.

I therefore employed the Difference-in-Differences (DiD) estimator, treating the 2019-2020 market boom (and subsequent COVID-19 supply chain shock) as an exogenous shock. This "Natural Experiment" design allows us to isolate the specific causal effect of the market shift on B2B companies.

The core of my analysis relies on the following regression model:

$Y_{it} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 Treat_i + \beta_2 Post_t + \beta_3 (Treat_i \times Post_t) + \epsilon_{it}$

Where:

  • $Treat_i$ captures the inherent differences between B2B and B2C groups.
  • $Post_t$ captures the time trend affecting all companies after 2019.
  • $\beta_3 (Treat \times Post)$ is the coefficient of interest. This Interaction Term measures the "excess" growth B2B companies experienced specifically because of the pivot, relative to the control group.

Cleaning the Data

The financial data in the 'RaisedToDate' column was stored as strings containing dollar signs and commas (e.g., "$1,000,000"), which prevents numerical analysis. I wrote a helper function to strip these characters and handle missing values, converting everything into floating-point numbers.

def clean_currency(x):
    if isinstance(x, str):
        x = x.replace('$', '').replace(',', '')
        if x.strip() in ['-', '']: return None
        try: return float(x)
        except: return None
    return x

df['RaisedToDate_cleaned'] = df['RaisedToDate'].apply(clean_currency)

Categorizing Business Models & Defining Cohorts

A major challenge in this dataset was the presence of "Hybrid" companies—those that sell both ingredients to other businesses and branded products to consumers. Including these would muddy the distinction between treatment and control groups. To ensure a clean identification strategy, I strictly categorized companies as either 'B2B' or 'B2C', excluding any ambiguity.

def categorize_b2b(val):
    if pd.isna(val): return None
    if 'B2B' in val and 'B2C' in val: return None # Strict separation
    if 'B2B' in val: return 'B2B'
    if 'B2C' in val: return 'B2C'
    return None

df['BusinessModel'] = df['B2B/B2C'].apply(categorize_b2b)

Furthermore, to avoid capturing the noise of hundreds of tiny startups that emerged during the bubble, I filtered the data to create a consistent Cohort of companies founded on or before 2015. This allows us to track how established players navigated the market pivot.

def clean_year(y):
    try: return int(y)
    except: return None

df['Year_founded_clean'] = df['Year founded'].apply(clean_year)

# Filter for the established cohort
cohort_df = df[
    (df['Year_founded_clean'] <= 2015) & 
    (df['BusinessModel'].notnull()) & 
    (df['RaisedToDate_cleaned'].notnull())
].copy()

Regression Analysis and Counterfactual Plotting

To run the classical DiD regression, I created dummy variables for the treatment group (`Treat`) and the post-2019 period (`Post`). I also applied a logarithmic transformation to the capital raised. This is standard practice in financial econometrics because funding amounts often follow a power law, and log-transformation normalizes the distribution and reduces the impact of massive outliers.

cohort_df['Treat'] = (cohort_df['BusinessModel'] == 'B2B').astype(int)
cohort_df['Post'] = (cohort_df['Year'] >= 2020).astype(int)
cohort_df['Log_Raised'] = np.log1p(cohort_df['RaisedToDate_cleaned'])

# Running the OLS model with the interaction term
model = smf.ols("Log_Raised ~ Treat * Post", data=cohort_df).fit()
print(model.summary())

To visualize the results, I generated a plot that explicitly shows the Counterfactual. This dashed line represents "what would have happened" to the B2B companies if they had continued to follow the B2C growth trend, assuming the shock never occurred. The gap between the actual B2B line and this counterfactual line visualizes the causal effect.

Discussion of Results

The analysis reveals two critical insights that validate the "Picks and Shovels" hypothesis.

1. Validation of Parallel Trends:
Looking at the period from 2012 to 2019 (the left side of the graph), we observe that the fundraising trajectories of B2B and B2C companies moved largely in sync. This "Parallel Trend" is the most important assumption in DiD analysis. It confirms that, prior to the shock, market forces affected both business models similarly, making B2C a valid control group for this experiment.

2. The Divergence (The Causal Effect):
After 2019, we see a sharp divergence. While the B2C group continued on its previous trajectory (or grew modestly), the B2B group experienced an explosive acceleration in funding. The regression results confirm this with a positive and significant coefficient on the interaction term ($\beta_3$).

This "gap"—visualized by the shaded region in Figure 1—represents capital that flowed specifically into infrastructure and ingredients technology, above and beyond the general market growth. This suggests that the "smart money" in the industry correctly identified that the bottleneck was no longer creating new brands, but rather scaling the underlying production technology.
